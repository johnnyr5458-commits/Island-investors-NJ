## Where This All Started: NJ PACT

To get what's going on with the new flood rules, it helps to know where they came from.

A few years back, the state launched a broader initiative called NJ PACT — short for New Jersey Protecting Against Climate Threats. It's basically the umbrella program the Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) has been working under to update older land-use, flood, and stormwater rules that were written before modern climate data even existed.

A lot of New Jersey's existing flood standards were built on rainfall data collected through the 70s, 80s, and 90s.

The state's thinking is pretty simple:

Storms have changed, sea levels have shifted, and rules built around the old numbers don't really reflect what's actually happening now.

Out of NJ PACT came two big pieces that are reshaping real estate conversations:

1. The Inland Flood Protection Rule
2. The REAL rule, which introduced the Climate-Adjusted Flood Elevation (CAFE) standards

Both deserve a calm, honest explanation.

## The Inland Flood Protection Rule

This was the first major piece, and it's the one most homeowners have already bumped into without realizing it — usually when trying to permit a project anywhere near a river, stream, or floodplain.

Here's what it does in plain English:

- Raises design flood elevation requirements in many inland flood hazard areas
- Uses updated climate-informed precipitation projections
- Requires higher elevations for certain structures
- Expands stormwater management rules
- Updates outdated runoff calculation standards

The rule mainly applies to new construction, substantial improvements, and certain redevelopment projects.

Here's the part that catches a lot of people off guard:

Some properties that were technically outside FEMA flood maps are now considered inside regulated flood hazard areas under DEP definitions.

That doesn't automatically mean flood insurance is required.

But it absolutely can change what gets required if somebody wants to build, expand, or heavily renovate a property.

That's where a lot of the confusion is coming from right now.

## The REAL Rule and the CAFE Standard

The REAL rule is the coastal companion to the Inland Flood Protection Rule, and it's the bigger one for South Jersey.

At the center of it is the Climate-Adjusted Flood Elevation standard.

In simple terms: some properties in coastal and tidal areas may now need to be built significantly higher than FEMA alone would normally require.

What that means in real life is:

- future renovations may cost more
- some projects may need additional engineering
- permitting timelines may stretch out
- substantial improvements can trigger compliance requirements

For investors, it means:

- renovation costs may be higher than they used to be
- permitting and engineering can materially change deal numbers
- projects near water may involve longer timelines and more review

## What This Means for South Jersey

Atlantic County, Cape May County, Ocean County, and surrounding shore communities sit right in the middle of this shift.

Low-lying coastal towns, bay-adjacent neighborhoods, and properties near tidal waterways are the most directly affected.

Some of the things people are already running into:

- higher construction costs
- longer permitting timelines
- more engineering documentation
- tighter underwriting from buyers and rehabbers
- questions about insurability and resale value

People have a lot more to consider these days.

But none of this means South Jersey real estate is suddenly falling apart.

It just means both homeowners and investors need better information before making decisions.

## The Flood Disclosure Law

New Jersey also strengthened flood disclosure requirements.

Sellers and landlords now have to disclose:

- certain FEMA flood zone information
- known flooding history
- prior flood damage
- flood insurance requirements
- previous flood assistance claims

For homeowners, flood history isn't really something that can quietly stay hidden anymore.

For investors, due diligence starts before the offer, not after.

Honestly, that's probably better for everyone involved.

## Why Investors Are Becoming More Careful

If you've gotten an offer recently and felt like investors were asking more questions than they used to, this is part of the reason.

Buyers now have to think about:

- elevation requirements
- DEP review
- engineering costs
- stormwater regulations
- insurance concerns
- longer rehab timelines

A responsible investor should be able to explain how they reached their number. Especially now, when renovation costs, elevation requirements, and permitting timelines can change a deal fast.

Transparency matters on both sides.

## Why Homeowners Shouldn't Panic

Worth saying clearly:

None of this means homes in South Jersey are suddenly worthless or unsellable.

A lot of these rules mainly affect:

- new construction
- major redevelopment
- substantial renovations

Not people quietly living in their homes.

The right response to all this isn't fear.

It's information.

Because clarity beats uncertainty every single time when it comes to making a decision.
